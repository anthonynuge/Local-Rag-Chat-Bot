# Thornmere Trail Guide

## Trailheads and access

All maintained trails start from one of three trailheads. The Gorge trailhead
sits directly behind the Fernline Visitor Center at the South Gate. The
Lakeshore trailhead is at the Larkspur Campground day-use lot, a 10-minute
Ridge Runner shuttle ride from the South Gate. The Sable trailhead is on the
Coldpine road, 4 km south of Coldpine Gate, and has the smallest parking area
in the park — on summer weekends it typically fills before 08:00, and the
shuttle is the reliable way in. Overnight parking is allowed only at the
Sable trailhead and requires the backcountry permit hang-tag on the dash.

Trail junctions are signed with the trail name and a numbered marker post;
the numbers match the posts printed on the TrailPass map layer, and rangers
use them to describe locations on the radio ("injured hiker at Sable 14").

## South District trails

The two southern trails are the park's most walked and are usually snow-free
from May onward.

### Gorge Rim Loop

The Gorge Rim Loop circles the upper rim of the Graywater canyon with three
viewpoint platforms over the falls. The full loop is **6.2 km** with 190 m of
total climbing, walked comfortably in about two hours. The rim sections are
fenced, but the connector down to the river put-in is not — keep children
close past marker post Gorge 7. The loop is the only trail in the park graded
for all-terrain wheelchairs as far as the second viewpoint (2.1 km out and
back on the same surface).

### Mirrorbend Lakeshore Path

A flat gravel path along the north shore of Mirrorbend Lake between the
Larkspur day-use lot and the canoe launch, **3.8 km** each way. This is the
only trail in Thornmere where dogs are permitted (leashed, as set out in the
visitor FAQ). Interpretive panels cover the 1978 establishment story and the
lake's formation. The path floods in sections during June snowmelt in wet
years; check the board at the trailhead.

## North District trails

The northern trails climb into the High Country zone. Both are serious
day-or-overnight undertakings, and overnight camping along them needs a
backcountry permit.

### Sable Ridge Trail

The Sable Ridge Trail runs from the Sable trailhead along the crest toward
the Thornmere massif: **11.4 km one-way with 780 m of elevation gain**,
typically 5.5 hours up at a steady pace. The route is exposed above treeline
from roughly km 6, with no water sources after Sable Spring at km 4.8 — carry
capacity for the whole ridge in summer. Designated backcountry sites sit at
km 7.9 (Sable Camp, four tent pads) and at the ridge shoulder at km 10.6
(Shoulder Camp, two pads). Afternoon lightning is the main seasonal hazard;
parties should plan to be off the crest by 14:00 on convective days.

### Thornmere Summit Route

From the end of the Sable Ridge Trail, the Summit Route continues **4.6 km**
to the top of Mount Thornmere, gaining a further 610 m over talus and two
short scrambling steps (no fixed protection; helmets recommended). It is a
route, not a maintained trail — cairned but unsigned past the Shoulder Camp
junction. The route is typically snow-free only from **mid-July to late
September**; outside that window, ice axe and crampon skills are expected,
and rangers log significantly more incident responses. There is no camping on
the summit plateau.

## Seasonal closures and conditions

The Summit Route closes entirely when the Meridian Parks Authority issues a
winter storm advisory for the High Country zone, and the closure is posted at
the Sable trailhead board and on the TrailPass conditions layer the same day.
The Lakeshore Path's flooded sections are signed rather than closed. A
section of the Gorge Rim Loop between posts Gorge 9 and Gorge 11 closes for
two weeks most Junes for peregrine nesting — dates vary by year and are set
by the wildlife program. Check current conditions at the Fernline desk or on
TrailPass before committing to the ridge.
