# Thornmere National Park — Overview

## What the park is

Thornmere National Park is a fictional 88,000-hectare mountain park managed by
the Meridian Parks Authority since its establishment in 1978. The park protects
the upper Graywater River watershed, the subalpine forests around Mirrorbend
Lake, and the exposed granite of the Thornmere massif, whose highest point,
Mount Thornmere, reaches 2,914 meters. The park exists in this knowledge base
as a self-contained reference: policies, trails, fees, and operations described
here are the authoritative version for visitors and staff.

The park's stated management goal is "wild country within a day's reach":
keeping the backcountry quiet and lightly developed while concentrating
services at the two road corridors. Roughly 90 percent of the park is managed
as backcountry with no vehicle access.

## Geography and zones

Park operations divide Thornmere into four named zones, and staff use these
names on the radio, in incident reports, and in closure notices:

- **Gorge zone** — the lower Graywater River canyon near the South Gate,
  including the Gorge Rim Loop and the river put-ins. Busiest zone; day-use
  dominated.
- **Lakeshore zone** — Mirrorbend Lake, its beach, the canoe launch, and
  Larkspur Campground. Family traffic and the concession dock.
- **High Country zone** — everything above 2,100 meters, including Sable Ridge
  and the Thornmere summit. Quota-limited overnight zone; bear canisters are
  mandatory here (see the permits guide).
- **Coldpine Forest zone** — the drier northern forest around Coldpine Gate
  and Coldpine Campground. Winter operations stage from here until the
  seasonal gate closure.

## Campgrounds and lodging

There is no lodge inside the park. Larkspur Campground on the Mirrorbend
lakeshore has **140 sites**, all reservable through TrailPass. Coldpine
Campground near the north entrance has **52 sites**, held first-come,
first-served all season. Each campground has a wood-fired shower house,
potable water from May through October, and firewood sales at the host site.
The nearest indoor lodging is outside the South Gate in the town of Fern
Hollow, which is not managed by the park.

## Visitor statistics

Recent annual visitation, as counted at both gates:

| Year | Visits  | Peak month |
|------|---------|------------|
| 2023 | 411,200 | July       |
| 2024 | 437,900 | August     |
| 2025 | 452,600 | July       |

About 70 percent of visits enter through the South Gate. The Ridge Runner
shuttle, introduced in 2019, now carries roughly a quarter of summer visitors
between the gates and the main trailheads.

## Operating seasons and hours

The South Gate and the Fernline Visitor Center beside it operate year-round.
Visitor center hours are 09:00–17:00 daily from May through October, and
weekends only, 10:00–16:00, from November through April. The Coldpine Gate is
seasonal; its closure dates are listed in the visitor FAQ. Entrance stations
are staffed 07:00–19:00 in summer; outside staffed hours, day passes are sold
at self-serve kiosks that accept cards only.

## Administration and contacts

The park is led by Superintendent **Dana Whitlock**, based at the Fernline
administrative annex. Chief Ranger **Ruben Ortega** heads the ranger division,
including search and rescue. The wildlife program is led by **Dr. Imani
Cole**. The concession for canoe rental and the lakeshore snack stand is held
by Summit & Pine Outfitters under a five-year agreement.

General inquiries go through the Fernline Visitor Center desk. The 24-hour
dispatch line for emergencies inside the park is **+1-555-0197**; dispatch
monitors Ridgenet radio channel 1 at all times. Media inquiries are handled
only by the superintendent's office, not by field staff.
