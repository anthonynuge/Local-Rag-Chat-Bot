Thornmere National Park wildlife safety guidance. This plain-text page is the visitor-facing summary maintained by the wildlife program; it is the version handed out at the gates and posted at both campground boards.

Black bears are active throughout the park from April through November. The rule that prevents nearly all conflict is food storage: in the frontcountry campgrounds, all food, coolers, cookware, toiletries, and any scented item must be stored in the metal food lockers provided at every site or inside a hard-sided vehicle whenever the items are not in immediate use. In the backcountry, food and scented items must be stored at least 60 meters from your tent, and in the High Country zone that storage must be a hard-sided bear canister as required by the backcountry permit conditions. Coolers are not bear-resistant and a locked car with visible food inside will draw a bear to the parking area; put it in the locker. A bear that obtains human food even once is likely to return, and the outcomes of that pattern are bad for the bear every time.

If you encounter a black bear on a trail, stop, group up, and speak in a calm firm voice while backing away slowly. Do not run and do not drop your pack — the pack protects your back and running invites a chase. If a bear approaches persistently, make yourselves large, shout, and throw objects toward (not at) it. Bear spray is legal and recommended in Thornmere; carry it accessible on a belt or chest holster, not inside the pack, and know the 8-meter effective range before you need it. Report every close encounter to the dispatch line at +1-555-0197 or at either visitor desk, even when nothing went wrong; the wildlife program maps encounter reports to catch developing problems early.

Moose are the park's most dangerous large animal in terms of visitor injuries, ahead of bears. Give any moose at least 25 meters, and much more during the fall rut from September 15 through October 31 and when a cow has a calf in spring. A moose with ears pinned back and hackles raised is about to charge; get behind a tree — a solid trunk defeats a moose in a way it does not defeat a bear. Most moose incidents at Thornmere happen on the Mirrorbend Lakeshore Path when visitors press close for photographs. No photograph is worth it.

Mountain lions are present but sightings are rare, averaging a handful of confirmed reports per year, mostly in the Coldpine Forest zone at dawn and dusk. Keep children within arm's reach on trails in that zone at those hours. If you meet a lion, do not crouch and do not run: maintain eye contact, appear large, back away slowly, and fight back with everything available if attacked. Report any sighting the same day.

Smaller hazards cause more actual incidents than the large animals. Ticks are active May through September in the grassy margins of the Gorge zone; treat clothing with permethrin or wear long trousers, and do a full check the same evening. Ground squirrels and gray jays at the viewpoints have learned to raid unattended food, and feeding them is not harmless: it concentrates predators, spreads disease within the colonies, and habituates animals to people.

Feeding any wildlife in Thornmere National Park, deliberately or through negligent food storage, is a citable offense with a fine of $175 per incident. Repeat or egregious cases, including luring wildlife for photography, can be referred for prosecution with substantially higher penalties and a park ban. Reports of visitors feeding animals are treated the same as any other wildlife incident: call the dispatch line, note the location by trail marker post if possible, and let the wildlife program follow up.

Drone use disturbs nesting raptors and ungulates and is prohibited park-wide; the permits guide covers the narrow research exception. Seasonal wildlife closures, such as the peregrine nesting closure on part of the Gorge Rim Loop most Junes, are posted on TrailPass and at trailhead boards the same day they are ordered. Respect the closure tape — the closures are short, specific, and the reason a visitor can still watch peregrines over the gorge at all.
