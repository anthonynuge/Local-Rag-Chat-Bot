# Thornmere Trail Guide

## Trailheads and access

All maintained trails start from one of three trailheads. The Gorge trailhead sits directly behind the Fernline Visitor Center at the South Gate, with a paved lot shared with visitor center parking; it is the easiest trailhead to reach on foot for anyone staying at Fern Hollow. The Lakeshore trailhead is at the Larkspur Campground day-use lot, a 10-minute Ridge Runner shuttle ride from the South Gate, and doubles as the put-in area for canoe rentals from Summit & Pine Outfitters, so the lot can feel crowded on warm afternoons even though hiker parking is a separate marked section. The Sable trailhead is on the Coldpine road, 4 km south of Coldpine Gate, and has the smallest parking area in the park — on summer weekends it typically fills before 08:00, and the shuttle is the reliable way in; hikers who drive and find the lot full should not park along the road shoulder, since the shoulder is narrow gravel over a drainage ditch and vehicles there have been ticketed and, in a few cases, towed. Overnight parking is allowed only at the Sable trailhead and requires the backcountry permit hang-tag on the dash, displayed face-up on the dashboard for the full duration of the trip.

Each trailhead has a signboard with the current conditions sheet, a trail map keyed to the marker-post numbers, and a steel registration box for day hikers to log their planned route and return time — filling out the slip is voluntary but strongly encouraged, since it is the first thing rangers check if a search begins. The registration slips are carbonless duplicates: the hiker keeps one copy and drops the other in the box, and rangers sweep the boxes each evening during the closing patrol, discarding slips for parties that signed back out. The conditions sheet is updated by whichever ranger last patrolled that trail and is dated at the top; a sheet more than a few days old during a stretch of unsettled weather is a cue to check TrailPass rather than assume nothing has changed. The Gorge and Lakeshore trailheads have vault toilets; the Sable trailhead has none, and the nearest facilities are back at the Coldpine Gate kiosk, roughly a ten-minute drive from the trailhead lot.

Trail junctions are signed with the trail name and a numbered marker post; the numbers match the posts printed on the TrailPass map layer, and rangers use them to describe locations on the radio ("injured hiker at Sable 14"). Marker posts are spaced roughly every 400–600 m along each trail, more tightly near junctions and viewpoints, and are numbered sequentially outward from the trailhead — so lower numbers are always closer to the parking area. Each post carries the trail's short code (Gorge, Lake, or Sable — the Summit Route shares the "Sable" code since it is reached by continuing past the ridge's final post) stamped into a weatherproof aluminum tag, along with the post number and, at junctions, an arrow toward each connecting trail. Posts are cedar, roughly waist height, set in a concrete collar to resist frost heave, and are replaced on a rolling basis when winter frost-heave or deadfall damages them — a missing or leaning post is worth reporting to the Fernline desk but is not by itself a sign of closure. On the two southern trails a leaning or knocked-down post is usually fixed within a few weeks of trail-crew notice; on the Sable Ridge Trail, where crew access is longer, a damaged post above treeline can take most of a season to reach, which is one reason the trailhead conditions sheet calls out known post gaps by number.

Cell coverage is effectively nonexistent beyond the gates, so none of the three trailheads should be treated as a point where a dropped call can be regained partway along a trail; hikers wanting a communication option for a Sable Ridge or Summit Route trip typically rent a satellite messenger in Fern Hollow before driving in, since none are available at any trailhead or park facility. A paper map or a downloaded offline TrailPass map layer is the practical navigation tool inside the park; posted marker numbers are the fastest way to describe a location to a ranger by radio or, once back in range, by phone, and hikers are encouraged to note the last post number they passed whenever they stop for a break on the northern trails.

## Difficulty ratings

Thornmere uses a four-level difficulty scale posted on every trailhead board and printed on the TrailPass map layer:

- **Easy** — mostly flat or gently graded, well-defined tread, suitable for most fitness levels and, where noted, wheelchair users. Both South District trails are rated Easy.
- **Moderate** — sustained climbing or descent, some uneven or rocky tread, appropriate for hikers with prior trail experience. No trail in the current network carries this rating on its own, though the lower kilometers of Sable Ridge Trail (below the treeline exposure at km 6) feel closer to Moderate than to the trail's overall Difficult rating.
- **Difficult** — significant elevation gain, exposed sections, longer duration, and route-finding demands beyond simply following a marked tread. The Sable Ridge Trail carries this rating for its full length.
- **Scramble** — hands-on-rock travel, unmaintained or unsigned sections, and terrain where a fall carries real consequence. Only the Thornmere Summit Route carries this rating; it is treated as a mountaineering objective rather than a maintained trail.

The rating reflects the trail's hardest representative section, not its average, and does not adjust for weather — a trail rated Easy in good conditions can become hazardous in the conditions described below. The trailhead signboards print the rating alongside a short plain-language note ("exposed above treeline," "no dogs," "wheelchair accessible to viewpoint 2") so a hiker checking the board on a whim, without a map in hand, still gets the gist of what the rating implies for that specific trail.

## South District trails

The two southern trails are the park's most walked and are usually snow-free from May onward.

### Gorge Rim Loop

The Gorge Rim Loop circles the upper rim of the Graywater canyon with three viewpoint platforms over the falls. The full loop is **6.2 km** with 190 m of total climbing, walked comfortably in about two hours. The tread is packed gravel and boardwalk for the accessible section near the trailhead, changing to a narrower dirt-and-root path with occasional exposed bedrock past the second viewpoint. The rim sections are fenced, but the connector down to the river put-in is not — keep children close past marker post Gorge 7. The loop is the only trail in the park graded for all-terrain wheelchairs as far as the second viewpoint (2.1 km out and back on the same surface); the gradient on that stretch never exceeds roughly 5%, and there is a hardened turnaround pad at the viewpoint itself.

Posts Gorge 1 through Gorge 6 cover the outbound rim section to the first two viewpoints; Gorge 7 marks the unfenced river-put-in connector; Gorge 8 sits at the third and highest viewpoint, where the trail turns away from the rim and back through drier forest to close the loop at Gorge 12, a short walk from the trailhead. Between Gorge 8 and Gorge 12 the tread drops away from the canyon edge and the fencing ends, since the trail is no longer running along an exposed drop; this forested closing stretch is the shadiest part of the loop and is a common spot to see gray jays working the ground for dropped food. There is no reliable water source along the loop — day hikers are expected to carry what they need rather than rely on the Graywater itself, which is fast and cold at the rim and, in any case, is off-limits to entry since swimming in the gorge pools is prohibited year-round. The main hazards are the unfenced connector edge and, in early morning, slick boardwalk sections near the first viewpoint after overnight condensation or rain; the bedrock sections past the second viewpoint can also be slick when wet, and hikers in trail runners rather than grippier footwear should slow down through that stretch. The three viewpoint platforms are built out over the rim on cantilevered decking with a solid guardrail, and each has room for a handful of parties at once, though the third viewpoint at Gorge 8 is noticeably smaller than the first two and can back up briefly on busy midday weekends.

### Mirrorbend Lakeshore Path

A flat gravel path along the north shore of Mirrorbend Lake between the Larkspur day-use lot and the canoe launch, **3.8 km** each way. This is the only trail in Thornmere where dogs are permitted (leashed, as set out in the visitor FAQ). Interpretive panels cover the 1978 establishment story and the lake's formation, spaced at roughly every half-kilometer along the shore side of the path. The path floods in sections during June snowmelt in wet years; check the board at the trailhead. Marker posts Lake 1 through Lake 9 run the length of the path at roughly even spacing, with Lake 5 marking the midpoint beach access spur and Lake 9 at the canoe launch turnaround. Between Lake 1 and Lake 5 the path stays within sight of the water the whole way and is the busiest stretch, since it covers the swimming beach and the interpretive panels most families stop to read; past Lake 5 the crowd thins noticeably, and the final approach to Lake 9 runs through a stand of taller conifers set slightly back from the shoreline before opening onto the canoe launch dock. The gravel surface and near-zero grade make this the easiest trail in the park to walk in the dark with a headlamp, and it is the one most commonly used for an easy evening out-and-back from Larkspur Campground. Because it is also the trail with the most recorded moose encounters in the park, hikers walking it at dawn, dusk, or after dark are asked to make ordinary trail noise rather than approach quietly, particularly along the brushier sections between Lake 6 and Lake 8 where the path pulls back from open shoreline into denser willow.

## North District trails

The northern trails climb into the High Country zone. Both are serious day-or-overnight undertakings, and overnight camping along them needs a backcountry permit.

### Sable Ridge Trail

The Sable Ridge Trail runs from the Sable trailhead along the crest toward the Thornmere massif: **11.4 km one-way with 780 m of elevation gain**, typically 5.5 hours up at a steady pace. The first four kilometers switchback through forest on a well-defined dirt tread with occasional root and rock steps; posts Sable 1 through Sable 6 mark this section, including Sable 5 at the Sable Spring water source (km 4.8) — the last reliable water on the trail. Sable Spring itself is a small piped seep with a metal cup chained to the post; flow slows to a trickle by late summer in a dry year, so hikers passing through August should treat it as a supplement rather than a guaranteed refill and top off earlier if the trailhead sheet flags low flow. Above roughly km 6 (post Sable 8, the treeline marker) the trail leaves the treeline and follows an exposed rocky crest the rest of the way, marked by posts Sable 9 through Sable 14; Sable 11 sits near Sable Camp, and Sable 14 sits at Shoulder Camp and the junction with the Thornmere Summit Route. The route is exposed above treeline from roughly km 6, with no water sources after Sable Spring at km 4.8 — carry capacity for the whole ridge in summer, since even a clear morning can turn into a long, hot exposed traverse by early afternoon.

Between Sable 8 and Sable 11 the crest narrows in places to a single-file tread with loose scree on either shoulder, and the views open up fully — Mirrorbend Lake is visible below to the southwest on a clear day, and the Thornmere massif dominates the skyline ahead. This stretch is also where the trail is most exposed to wind; gusts funneling along the ridge can be noticeably stronger than what a hiker felt lower down, and a light jacket that felt unnecessary at the trailhead is routinely welcome by Sable 9. Designated backcountry sites sit at km 7.9 (Sable Camp, four tent pads, near post Sable 11) and at the ridge shoulder at km 10.6 (Shoulder Camp, two pads, at post Sable 14); both camps have a wind-sheltered cooking area — a low rock-walled nook set slightly off the tent pads — but no privy, so High Country sanitation follows a pack-it-out or cathole standard away from the tent pads and the trail. At Sable Camp the four pads are set among low, wind-flagged trees just below the true crest, giving the site more shelter than Shoulder Camp's two pads, which sit fully exposed on the open shoulder and catch whatever wind is running the ridge that night; parties sensitive to wind and cold generally prefer Sable Camp for that reason, even though Shoulder Camp shaves distance off a summit attempt the next morning. Neither camp has a water source of its own — anyone camping at either site needs to have carried water up from Sable Spring, since there is nothing to refill from at km 7.9 or km 10.6. Afternoon lightning is the main seasonal hazard; parties should plan to be off the crest by 14:00 on convective days, and the trailhead conditions sheet typically flags any run of afternoon thunderstorm activity so hikers can plan an earlier start.

### Thornmere Summit Route

From the end of the Sable Ridge Trail, the Summit Route continues **4.6 km** to the top of Mount Thornmere, gaining a further 610 m over talus and two short scrambling steps (no fixed protection; helmets recommended). It is a route, not a maintained trail — cairned but unsigned past the Shoulder Camp junction, meaning hikers navigate cairn to cairn rather than following a worn tread; in low visibility the cairns can be hard to relocate, and turning back is the standard advice if two consecutive cairns cannot be found. The first scrambling step comes roughly a third of the way up from Shoulder Camp, a short hand-and-foot move up a broken rock band that is the more exposed of the two; the second step, higher up, is shorter but sits on looser rock, and parties are advised to test handholds before committing weight. Between the two steps the route crosses a long field of ankle-to-knee-sized talus where the going is slow and cairns are spaced closer together than on the smoother ground below. The route is typically snow-free only from **mid-July to late September**; outside that window, ice axe and crampon skills are expected, and rangers log significantly more incident responses. There is no camping on the summit plateau, and because the route starts at Shoulder Camp rather than at its own trailhead, most parties treat the summit as a long day extension of a Sable Ridge overnight rather than as a stand-alone outing.

## Day-hike and overnight guidance

The two South District trails are straightforward day hikes from either the Gorge or Lakeshore trailhead and need no permit. The Sable Ridge Trail can be walked as a long out-and-back day hike from the Sable trailhead by a fit party starting early, but most visitors split it into an overnight using Sable Camp or Shoulder Camp, which requires a High Country backcountry permit and a bear canister as described in the park's permit and wildlife material. Reaching the summit in a single day from the Sable trailhead is possible only for a fast party starting well before sunrise, given the combined 16 km and 1,390 m of climbing involved in trailhead-to-summit travel; most who want the summit plan two days, camping at Shoulder Camp and making the final push light the next morning, carrying only water, layers, and a small amount of food rather than the full overnight kit for the summit push itself.

A typical two-day Sable itinerary starts at the trailhead by mid-morning, reaches Sable Camp or Shoulder Camp by mid-to-late afternoon depending on pace and chosen camp, and leaves the second day free for either a pre-dawn summit push (timed to be off the exposed scrambling sections before any afternoon weather builds) or a more relaxed return hike out. Parties camping at Sable Camp rather than Shoulder Camp add roughly 2.7 km and a short additional climb to a summit-day round trip, which is the main trade-off against Sable Camp's better wind shelter. Because neither camp has water, most overnight parties fill to capacity at Sable Spring on the way up rather than planning a return trip down to km 4.8 mid-stay, and pace their carry accordingly on the switchbacks below treeline.

## Trail etiquette and right-of-way

- Uphill hikers have the right-of-way; downhill hikers step aside at a suitable wide spot rather than asking an uphill party to yield mid-climb.
- On the Mirrorbend Lakeshore Path, where dogs are permitted, leashed dogs and their handlers yield the path edge to hikers with strollers or mobility devices.
- Cyclists are not permitted on any Thornmere trail; none of the four named trails are open to bikes, and the marker-post system does not include a bike-route designation.
- Hikers descending the Sable Ridge Trail's exposed crest yield to any party still climbing during the afternoon lightning window, since the descending party has more options to shelter below treeline.
- Pack out everything carried in, including on the maintained South District trails; there are no trash receptacles at any trailhead or along any trail, in line with the park-wide no-trash-cans policy outside South Gate and the campgrounds.
- Stay on the marked tread at all viewpoints and junctions — cutting switchbacks accelerates erosion on the granite-thin soils above treeline and is the single largest driver of trail-crew rework on Sable Ridge.
- Groups meeting at a marker post junction should let the smaller or faster party pass first rather than merging travel.
- On the narrow single-file sections of Sable Ridge between Sable 8 and Sable 11, the party on the downhill or outer side of the tread should be the one to step off onto stable ground, since the inner shoulder tends to be firmer and the outer shoulder is loose scree.
- At Sable Camp and Shoulder Camp, cooking should stay in the designated wind-sheltered nook rather than at the tent pads, both to keep food smells away from sleeping areas and to keep the limited flat ground at each pad free of gear during meal prep.
- Dog waste on the Lakeshore Path must be packed out; bags are not provided at the trailhead, so handlers should carry their own in.
- Voluntary trail-steward volunteers doing maintenance work (one weekend day per month, May through October) may have a section of tread flagged off briefly; hikers should follow their directions or take the short marked detour rather than push past active work.

## Seasonal closures and conditions

The Summit Route closes entirely when the Meridian Parks Authority issues a winter storm advisory for the High Country zone, and the closure is posted at the Sable trailhead board and on the TrailPass conditions layer the same day. The Lakeshore Path's flooded sections are signed rather than closed. A section of the Gorge Rim Loop between posts Gorge 9 and Gorge 11 closes for two weeks most Junes for peregrine nesting — dates vary by year and are set by the wildlife program. Check current conditions at the Fernline desk or on TrailPass before committing to the ridge.

Outside the mid-July to late-September snow-free window, the Summit Route is generally considered off-trail mountaineering terrain rather than a hiking route, and the Sable Ridge Trail itself can hold isolated snow patches on its shaded northern switchbacks well into June in a heavy snow year — the conditions sheet at the Sable trailhead notes any such lingering patches by marker post. In shoulder season (roughly October and again in April–May), the South District trails are typically the most reliable options, since the Gorge Rim Loop's lower elevation keeps it snow-free longest and the Lakeshore Path's flat gravel tread sheds moisture fastest. Shoulder-season mornings on the Gorge Rim Loop's boardwalk sections can carry frost or a thin skim of ice even when the surrounding tread is clear, so the same slick-surface caution that applies after summer rain applies doubly in October and April; the trailhead sheet flags icy boardwalk mornings when the overnight low has dropped near freezing. On the Lakeshore Path, shoulder-season storms occasionally drop small deadfall across the tread near the willow stretch between Lake 6 and Lake 8, which trail crews clear on their next scheduled pass rather than same-day, so a downed branch there is a minor detour rather than a sign of a bigger problem.

During Coldpine Gate's seasonal vehicle closure (Nov 1 – Apr 30), reaching the Sable trailhead requires travel on the ungroomed road beyond the gate, which functionally limits winter access to the Sable Ridge Trail and Summit Route to those equipped and self-registered for winter backcountry travel; the two South District trails remain reachable year-round from the South Gate. Winter travel on the lower Sable Ridge switchbacks is typically on snowshoes once snowpack builds up, and the exposed crest above treeline becomes a genuine winter mountaineering setting rather than an extension of the summer hiking trail — wind loading on the ridge's lee slopes creates avalanche-prone terrain that the marked summer tread does not account for, and self-registered winter parties are expected to have their own avalanche judgment rather than rely on trail signage, since none of the winter hazard information is posted trailside. The South District trails see occasional light winter use from South Gate day visitors on foot or snowshoe; the Gorge Rim Loop's fenced rim sections and the Lakeshore Path's flat grade make both comparatively forgiving in snow, though the Lakeshore Path's flooding-prone sections can instead ice over in a hard freeze, which is its own slip hazard distinct from the June flooding.

## Accessibility notes

The Gorge Rim Loop's accessible section — the 2.1 km out-and-back to the second viewpoint — is the park's only trail segment graded for all-terrain wheelchairs, with a maximum sustained grade of roughly 5%, a hardened gravel-and-boardwalk surface, and a turnaround pad at the viewpoint itself. The accessible parking spaces nearest the Gorge trailhead sit directly beside the vault toilet building, and the trailhead signboard marks the accessible route with its own symbol distinct from the general trail map so a visitor scanning the board can find it without reading the full text. The Mirrorbend Lakeshore Path's flat gravel tread is walkable by most mobility-device users as far as conditions allow, though it is not formally graded as an accessible route, and the June flooding noted above can make sections impassable for wheeled users even when still walkable for hikers; the beach access spur at Lake 5 has a short firmer-surface run down to the sand for anyone transferring to a beach wheelchair, though the park does not provide beach wheelchairs itself. Neither northern trail has any accessible grading, and the terrain on both — root-and-rock switchbacks below treeline, exposed scree above it — makes them unsuitable for wheeled mobility devices at any point along their length. Visitors planning an accessible outing who also want to camp should note that Larkspur Campground's six accessible sites and single accessible shower stall are the more suitable base, given the Lakeshore Path's proximity, over Coldpine's two accessible sites, which sit farther from either maintained trail with formal accessibility features.
