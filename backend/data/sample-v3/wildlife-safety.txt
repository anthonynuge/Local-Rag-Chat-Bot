Thornmere National Park wildlife safety guidance. This plain-text page is the visitor-facing summary maintained by the wildlife program; it is the version handed out at the gates and posted at both campground boards. It covers black bears, moose, mountain lions, the smaller hazards that actually cause most incidents, food storage, bear spray, the feeding-wildlife citation, what to do after any encounter, and a season-by-season activity calendar.

Black bears are active throughout the park from April through November. They emerge from winter dens in early spring hungry and travel widely looking for early greenery and carrion, then settle into a summer pattern of foraging along stream corridors and berry patches, and by late September shift into hyperphagia, the pre-den feeding push that keeps them moving and searching for calories right up until the season ends. A bear encountered in October is not calmer than one encountered in June; it is hungrier and more food-motivated, so storage discipline matters most at both ends of the season. Den sites are concentrated on north-facing slopes above roughly 1,900 meters, which puts most of the park's winter dens within the High Country and upper Coldpine Forest zones; a bear that dens close to a popular route is one reason the wildlife program checks known denning areas each March before the snowpack softens enough for visitors to reach them.

Bear behavior and storage discipline both shift by zone. In the Gorge zone, day-use crowding and picnic food are the main draw, and most Gorge bear activity is a bear working the margins near the visitor center dumpsters and picnic tables rather than approaching hikers directly; a bear seen here is usually moving through, not settling in, because the zone offers little natural forage past the canyon walls. The Lakeshore zone sees the heaviest bear traffic in berry season, roughly late July through early September, when huckleberry and serviceberry thickets along the shoreline draw bears within sight of the beach and the Larkspur day-use lot; rangers post temporary "bear in area" signs at the affected trail junctions rather than closing the whole path, since the animals are typically feeding and not aggressive. The High Country zone has the lowest bear density of the four but the highest consequence if food discipline fails, because a bear that finds an unattended pack near Sable Camp or Shoulder Camp has no competing food source for kilometers and will return to the same site on subsequent nights; this is the core reason the hard-sided canister requirement applies there and nowhere else. The Coldpine Forest zone, drier and more open than the Lakeshore or Gorge, sees bears concentrated along Fenwick Creek in early summer and again in fall as they follow the same drainage down from higher ground; Coldpine Campground's food lockers see more use-related maintenance calls than Larkspur's for exactly that reason.

The rule that prevents nearly all conflict is food storage. In the frontcountry campgrounds, all food, coolers, cookware, toiletries, and any scented item must be stored in the metal food lockers provided at every site or inside a hard-sided vehicle whenever the items are not in immediate use. This applies the same way at both Larkspur and Coldpine; the locker doors latch with a simple lever a bear cannot operate, but only if a visitor closes them fully, and rangers report that an unlatched-but-closed locker door is the single most common cause of a frontcountry bear getting into food. Host-site staff at both campgrounds do an evening locker sweep during the peak season, but that sweep is a backstop, not a substitute for closing your own. In the backcountry, food and scented items must be stored at least 60 meters from your tent, and in the High Country zone that storage must be a hard-sided bear canister as required by the backcountry permit conditions; hanging food from a tree is not an acceptable substitute anywhere above treeline because the terrain rarely offers a branch that defeats a bear. Below treeline, a proper hang still needs to clear the ground by at least 4 meters and sit at least 2 meters out from the trunk or any supporting branch, and it should be tied off to a separate anchor from the one you climbed to set it, so a bear working the trunk cannot shake the line loose. Choose a hang branch that is clearly too thin to bear a bear's weight if you can reach one; the point of the 4-meter and 2-meter figures is to put the food out of both a standing reach and a climbing reach at once. Canisters rented at Fernline for High Country trips should be packed with the odor-sealing lid oriented away from the tent door and set at least the same 60 meters away, laid on its side against a rock or root so it cannot roll downhill in the night. Wash dishes and dispose of grey water at the same 60-meter distance from camp, and never pour cooking grease or fish cleaning waste directly on the ground; pack it out with the rest of your food waste. Coolers are not bear-resistant and a locked car with visible food inside will draw a bear to the parking area; put it in the locker. A bear that obtains human food even once is likely to return, and the outcomes of that pattern are bad for the bear every time — a food-conditioned bear that keeps approaching people is typically relocated once and then, if the behavior continues, is not returned to the park, which is the exact outcome careful storage exists to prevent.

If you encounter a black bear on a trail, stop, group up, and speak in a calm firm voice while backing away slowly. Do not run and do not drop your pack — the pack protects your back and running invites a chase. If a bear approaches persistently, make yourselves large, shout, and throw objects toward (not at) it. Bear spray is legal and recommended in Thornmere; carry it accessible on a belt or chest holster, not inside the pack, and know the 8-meter effective range before you need it. Wait until the bear is within that range and closing before you deploy; spraying too early wastes the canister's roughly seven to nine seconds of discharge and leaves nothing for a second attempt. Aim slightly downward into the bear's path rather than directly at its face if there is any crosswind, so the cloud settles into the animal's route instead of drifting wide. Check the canister's printed expiration date before every trip — the propellant loses pressure over time — and practice drawing it from the holster at home so the motion is not the first thing you have to think about in an actual charge. If you are ever sprayed by backscatter or wind drift, flush your eyes and skin with clean water as soon as it is safe to do so; the effect is temporary and clears within about twenty to thirty minutes. In the rare case that a black bear makes contact despite these steps, the recommended response differs from what many visitors expect: fight back, targeting the face and muzzle, rather than playing dead, since playing dead is advice for a defensive grizzly-type encounter and not for the food-motivated or predatory behavior a black bear contact almost always represents. After any encounter that involved a charge, contact, or property damage, do not return to that section of trail; note the nearest numbered marker post and report it as described below.

Moose are the park's most dangerous large animal in terms of visitor injuries, ahead of bears. Give any moose at least 25 meters, and much more during the fall rut from September 15 through October 31 and when a cow has a calf in spring. A moose with ears pinned back and hackles raised is about to charge; get behind a tree — a solid trunk defeats a moose in a way it does not defeat a bear, since a moose will not climb or dig, it will simply give up and go around. Other warning signs worth learning include a lowered head with the nose tucked, a stiff stalking walk toward you rather than away, and licking of the lips, all of which typically precede a charge by only a few seconds, so the time to act on ear position and hackles is before those closer signs appear. During the rut, bull moose can also respond to human voices or even hiking poles clacking together as though they were a rival's antlers, so keep noise ordinary and non-confrontational rather than trying to haze a moose off a trail. If a moose does knock you down, curl into a ball, protect your head and neck with your arms, and stay still until it moves off; getting up too soon can trigger another charge, and most moose that have knocked a person down disengage within moments if the person stops moving. Most moose incidents at Thornmere happen on the Mirrorbend Lakeshore Path when visitors press close for photographs, and the interpretive panels along that path now carry a moose-specific reminder for exactly that reason. No photograph is worth it.

Mountain lions are present but sightings are rare, averaging a handful of confirmed reports per year, mostly in the Coldpine Forest zone at dawn and dusk. Signs of recent activity include fresh tracks showing four toes without claw marks and, occasionally, a partially covered cache of a deer carcass; if you find a cache, leave the area immediately by the way you came rather than continuing past it, since a lion defending a cache is far more likely to act defensively than one simply passing through. Other signs include scat containing hair and bone fragments and shallow scrape marks in soil or leaf litter used to mark territory; none of these on their own mean a lion is nearby at that moment, but a fresh cache is the one sign that calls for leaving immediately. Keep children within arm's reach on trails in that zone at those hours, and keep hiking groups together rather than letting anyone lag behind or range ahead alone, since a lion is far more likely to test a lone or small target than a tight group. Hiking in pairs or larger groups in the Coldpine Forest zone during the dawn and dusk hours is the single most effective precaution the wildlife program recommends. If you meet a lion, do not crouch and do not run: maintain eye contact, appear large by raising your arms or opening a jacket, speak in a loud firm voice, back away slowly, and fight back with everything available if attacked, aiming for the face and eyes. Pick up small children without turning your back or bending down fully, since crouching or turning away can trigger a chase response. Report any sighting the same day, whether or not the animal reacted to you.

Smaller hazards cause more actual incidents than the large animals. Ticks are active May through September in the grassy margins of the Gorge zone; treat clothing with permethrin or wear long trousers tucked into socks, and do a full check the same evening, paying particular attention to the scalp, behind the ears, the waistband line, and behind the knees. Light-colored clothing makes an unattached tick easier to spot before it settles in for a bite. Remove an attached tick with fine-tipped tweezers, pulling straight out at the skin without twisting, and watch the bite site for the next several weeks for an expanding red rash or flu-like symptoms; save the tick in a sealed bag with the date if you can, since it may help a clinician later even though the park does not test ticks itself. Ground squirrels and gray jays at the viewpoints have learned to raid unattended food, and feeding them is not harmless: it concentrates predators, spreads disease within the colonies, and habituates animals to people; a ground squirrel that has learned to associate a backpack with food will climb into an open one within seconds if a visitor turns away, and gray jays are bold enough to take food directly from an open hand or picnic table. A less obvious hazard in the same Gorge zone understory is bristlewood nettle, a low shrub with a pale ridged stem that flowers June through August; brushing bare skin against its leaves causes a stinging contact rash that usually resolves within a day, and it is easiest to identify by the faint silver cast on the underside of each leaf. A cool compress and avoiding further scratching are the only care most visitors need; if the rash spreads well beyond the contact area or blisters heavily, treat it like any other skin reaction that is not improving and seek care rather than waiting it out. Give it a wide step rather than pushing through undergrowth off the fenced sections of trail. Above treeline, thunderstorm exposure is its own hazard on top of any wildlife concern: the open ridgeline past kilometer 6 of the Sable Ridge Trail has no cover from lightning, which is the reason the guidance for that section is to be off the crest by 14:00 on convective-weather days, the same window that keeps hikers clear of both storm cells and the afternoon hours when local wildlife is least active and least predictable in its movement toward water.

What to do immediately after any encounter depends on whether anyone was hurt. If there is an injury from a bite, claw, kick, or fall during an encounter, treat it as you would any wilderness injury first: control bleeding with direct pressure and a clean dressing, immobilize an injured limb, and keep the person warm and as still as reasonably possible while you arrange help. Bites and scratches from any wild animal carry a real infection risk even when they look minor, so wash the wound with clean water as soon as you can and cover it, then get it evaluated the same day rather than waiting to see how it looks tomorrow. Call the dispatch line for anything beyond a minor scrape; for a serious injury more than a short walk from a trailhead, dispatch can begin the same search-and-rescue and evacuation process used for any other backcountry medical emergency. If no one was hurt, the priority is still to leave the immediate area calmly rather than continuing to observe or photograph the animal, and to make note of exactly where it happened before the details fade.

Feeding or luring wildlife — including baiting an animal closer for a photograph — is a citable offense with a $175 fine per incident; repeat or egregious cases are referred to the superintendent's office for prosecution and can result in a park ban. To report any bear, moose, or mountain lion encounter, a food-storage violation, or a sighting of feeding behavior, call the 24-hour dispatch line at +1-555-0197, or stop at either visitor desk during staffed hours. Give the trail or campground, the nearest marker post or site number, the time, and what the animal did; dispatch logs the report and routes it to the wildlife program for follow-up. A useful report is specific: which direction the animal was moving, how close it came, whether it reacted to your presence at all, and whether food or garbage was involved, since that last detail is often what determines whether the wildlife program treats it as an isolated sighting or the start of a food-conditioning pattern. Reporting matters even when the encounter ended without incident — a cluster of reports from the same stretch of trail over a few days is usually the first sign of a bear or lion pattern that needs an active response, and it can lead to a short trail closure while the wildlife program checks the area. Closures of this kind are posted on TrailPass and at the affected trailhead board the same day they are ordered, and they are lifted as soon as the wildlife program confirms the animal has moved on or the immediate concern has passed; visitors already on an affected trail when a closure goes up are not pulled off but are asked to leave by the nearest trailhead rather than continuing.

The park's wildlife activity follows a predictable seasonal pattern worth planning a visit around. In April, bears begin appearing at low elevations as snow retreats, moving slowly and still thin from winter; encounters are uncommon simply because visitor traffic is still light. May brings the start of tick season in the Gorge zone and the first steady bear sightings across all four zones as forage greens up. June adds moose calving through the month, which raises the stakes of any close approach to a cow, alongside the peregrine nesting closure that typically affects part of the Gorge Rim Loop for about two weeks. July and August are the peak overlap of visitor traffic and bear activity, with Lakeshore-zone berry feeding well underway by late July and continuing into early September; tick season is also at its most active across these two months. September opens with the last weeks of steady tick activity in the first half of the month, then shifts hard around the 15th into the fall rut, which runs through October 31 and is the single highest-risk window for moose encounters; it overlaps with bear hyperphagia in the same weeks, making mid-September through October the season when the wildlife program asks for the most caution on both fronts at once. October also sees bears at their most food-driven of the year as they push toward denning. By November, the rut has ended, bear activity is dropping off rapidly as animals begin denning on the north-facing slopes noted above, and the smaller-hazard season has closed out along with the ticks and bristlewood nettle's flowering window. From December through March, mountain lions are the primary ongoing wildlife consideration, since their activity in the Coldpine Forest zone is steady year-round and concentrated at dawn and dusk regardless of season, while bears remain den-bound and largely absent from the landscape until the pattern begins again in April.

Drone use disturbs nesting raptors and ungulates and is prohibited park-wide; the permits guide covers the narrow research exception. Seasonal wildlife closures, such as the peregrine nesting closure on part of the Gorge Rim Loop most Junes, are posted on TrailPass and at trailhead boards the same day they are ordered. Respect the closure tape — the closures are short, specific, and the reason a visitor can still watch peregrines over the gorge at all.
