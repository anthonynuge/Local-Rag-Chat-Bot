# Permits and Fees

## Entrance fees

Entrance fees are charged per vehicle or per person at both gates and at the self-serve kiosks outside staffed hours. Passes cover the calendar day of purchase, not 24 hours.

| Pass type                           | Price | Notes                              |
|-------------------------------------|-------|------------------------------------|
| Vehicle day pass                    | $22   | Covers everyone in the vehicle     |
| Per-person day pass (shuttle/bike/foot) | $9 | Under 12 free                    |
| Thornmere annual pass               | $65   | Named holder plus one vehicle      |
| Commercial van (up to 15 seats)     | $90   | Requires commercial registration   |
| Motorcycle day pass                 | $14   | Per machine                        |

The annual pass is sold at the Fernline Visitor Center desk and on TrailPass; gate staff cannot issue it. Fee-free days are announced each January by the Meridian Parks Authority — historically the park's establishment anniversary in June and one weekend in late September.

Staffed entrance stations at South Gate accept cash, chip-and-PIN cards, and the TrailPass mobile wallet. Self-serve kiosks outside staffed hours (07:00–19:00 in summer) are card-only; they do not accept cash and do not give change. A vehicle arriving before staffing begins can pay at the kiosk and continue in — there is no separate early-entry fee. Seniors (65+) and holders of a Meridian Parks Authority access pass for visitors with disabilities pay the standard per-person or vehicle rate; there is no separate discounted tier for either group at Thornmere. A lost or stolen annual pass can be reissued at the Fernline desk for a $10 replacement fee once the original holder's name is verified against the TrailPass account.

Card transactions at both the staffed station and the self-serve kiosk have a $5 minimum; a single-person foot entry at $9 clears that minimum on its own, but a card cannot be used to buy, for example, a $5 firewood bundle alone at the gate — firewood is sold at the campground host sites, not the entrance stations. Kiosks and staffed stations accept the major chip cards and contactless/mobile-wallet taps; they do not accept checks, foreign currency, or gift certificates. A declined card at a staffed station can be retried once; a vehicle that cannot produce valid payment is directed to the day-use turnaround area rather than held at the gate. Annual pass renewal within 30 days of the printed expiration date is available at the Fernline desk for $55 instead of the full $65, provided the name on the expiring pass matches the renewing visitor; renewals requested more than 30 days after expiration are treated as a new purchase at the full $65 rate. A returned or declined electronic payment discovered after the fact (for example a reversed charge on an annual pass) carries a $25 returned-payment fee in addition to the original amount owed, billed through the TrailPass account on file.

Commercial van operators must present proof of commercial registration matching the vehicle at the gate each visit; a van without that paperwork is charged the standard vehicle day pass rate per vehicle instead of the $90 commercial rate, since the discount is tied to registered commercial status, not seating capacity alone. Motorcycle passengers riding double are each covered under the single $14 motorcycle day pass, matching the vehicle-pass logic used for cars.

## Camping fees

Frontcountry camping is charged per site per night, with an eight-person, two-vehicle limit on standard sites.

| Site type                        | Price per night |
|-----------------------------------|-----------------|
| Larkspur standard site           | $28             |
| Larkspur lakeside site           | $36             |
| Coldpine standard site           | $18             |
| Group site (up to 24 people)     | $75             |

Larkspur sites are reservation-only through TrailPass. Coldpine is first-come, first-served, with self-registration envelopes at the host site; card payment at the host cabin is available 08:00–20:00. The two group sites are at Larkspur only and can be booked at most 12 months ahead.

A group-site booking requires a single named organizer who is responsible for the $75 fee and for every vehicle and tent associated with the reservation; the organizer must be present at check-in. Extra vehicles beyond the two-vehicle limit on a standard site can park in the Larkspur day-use lot at no additional charge, but overflow vehicles left overnight at an individual site are subject to the same enforcement as any other frontcountry rule violation. Firewood purchased at the host site is a separate transaction from the camping fee and is not bundled into any of the rates above.

Standard check-in at both campgrounds is 14:00 and checkout is 11:00; a site not vacated by 12:00 is treated as a second night and billed accordingly unless the host has approved a late checkout in advance. Quiet hours run 22:00–06:00 at both campgrounds; a repeated quiet-hours violation is handled the same way as any other citable frontcountry infraction, using the numbered triplicate citation form and referred to the regional court, with no field payment collected on site. Coldpine's self-registration envelope requires the license plate, number of nights, and site number written on the stub before it is dropped in the host-cabin box; a site occupied without a completed envelope or without card payment recorded by the next host round is flagged for a compliance check. A Coldpine site can be held only by physically placing the paid envelope stub at the site — reserving a specific site number in advance is not possible at Coldpine, unlike Larkspur.

Larkspur cancellations and date changes on a standard or lakeside site follow the same refund schedule described under Reservations, changes, and refunds below. A group-site organizer who needs to reduce the headcount after booking does not receive a partial refund, since the $75 rate is a flat site fee rather than a per-person charge; the fee stays fixed regardless of whether 5 or 24 people show up, as long as the total does not exceed the 24-person ceiling. A group that arrives over the 24-person ceiling is asked to reduce to the limit before check-in is completed; the overage does not get a second site automatically and must be booked and paid separately if a second group site is available on the same dates.

## Backcountry permits

Overnight stays anywhere outside the two campgrounds require a backcountry permit at **$12 per person per night**, booked on TrailPass. The High Country zone is under a nightly quota from May 15 through September 30; quota releases happen in two waves, 60 percent four months ahead and the rest seven days ahead. The **maximum group size on a single backcountry permit is 8 people**, and groups may not split a larger party across multiple permits to camp together — rangers check at the designated sites.

When the four-months-ahead release sells out for a given date, TrailPass adds names to a standby waitlist in the order they join it. Waitlisted parties are notified automatically, in waitlist order, as the seven-day release opens or as existing holders cancel; a waitlist offer must be accepted within 24 hours on the app or it passes to the next party in line. There is no fee to join a waitlist, and joining one does not reserve a canister rental — that must be requested separately once a permit is confirmed. Same-day walk-up nights, when they exist, are released at the Fernline desk starting at 07:00 on the day of travel and are drawn from any quota that went unclaimed at the 17:00 pickup deadline the day before.

Quota mechanics count by campsite bed-nights within the High Country zone, not by trail segment, so a party moving from Sable Camp to Shoulder Camp on consecutive nights consumes one quota slot at each site rather than one slot for the whole trip. A permit booked for a specific High Country night is tied to that calendar date and the named sites on it; a party that wants to add an extra night mid-trip must apply for an additional permit through the app if cell or satellite connectivity allows, or report the extension to a ranger on patrol, since silently overstaying a booked permit is treated as camping without a valid permit. The longest a single backcountry permit can run is 7 consecutive nights; a trip planned beyond that must be booked as two linked permits with no gap between them. A party of 8 that wants to camp as two separate tent groups at the same site on the same night still needs only one permit, since the 8-person cap applies to the party total rather than to tent count. Permits for the Gorge and Lakeshore zones, which are not quota-limited, can be booked any time within the six-month rolling window and do not compete for the same allocation as High Country nights.

A hard-sided bear canister is required for all food and scented items on every High Country overnight. Approved models are listed on TrailPass, and canisters can be rented at the Fernline Visitor Center for $5 per night with a $60 deposit. Hanging food is not an accepted alternative above treeline — there are no suitable trees. Canisters are checked out and returned in person at the Fernline desk during visitor center hours; the $60 deposit is held on a card and released once the canister is returned undamaged within 24 hours of the permit's end date. A canister returned late is charged an additional night's rental for each day overdue, deducted from the deposit; a canister that is not returned within seven days forfeits the full deposit and the renter is billed for replacement. Parties without their own gear are encouraged to reserve a canister on TrailPass at the same time as the backcountry permit, since the Fernline desk keeps a limited rental fleet and it can run out on the busiest quota-release weekends.

The Fernline rental fleet holds 45 canisters; each unit carries a barcode sticker that is scanned out against the renter's TrailPass account and scanned back in at return, which is how the 24-hour return clock and the late-fee count are tracked without manual logbooks. A canister returned visibly damaged — a cracked latch, a chewed gasket, a lid that no longer seals — is treated the same as an unreturned canister for billing purposes: the full deposit is kept and the renter is billed for replacement, since a compromised unit cannot be re-rented. Canisters are wiped down and spot-checked by the desk staff between rentals; a renter who receives a unit with a pre-existing scent or residue should flag it at pickup so the desk can swap it before the party leaves, since scent contamination is not grounds for a fee waiver once the party has already been on the trail. Personal canisters that meet the TrailPass-approved-model list do not need to be inspected or registered before a trip, but a party carrying an unlisted canister model can be asked by a ranger to demonstrate that it is bear-resistant, and food stored in a non-approved container is treated as unprotected food for enforcement purposes.

Permits must be picked up in person or activated on the TrailPass app by 17:00 on the start date, or the booking releases back to the quota. Digital activation on the app is accepted for every zone except the High Country quota season, when a party heading above 2,100 m must also collect a paper trip plan and canister tag (if renting) from the Fernline desk in person, since that copy is what search and rescue references if the party is overdue. The permit hang-tag must be displayed on the dash for overnight parking at the Sable trailhead.

A permit found to be missing, altered, or shared with a party other than the one it was issued to is treated as camping without a valid permit and is handled with the same numbered triplicate citation used for other civil infractions, referred to the regional court rather than settled in the field. A ranger checking permits at a designated backcountry site compares the names and headcount on the paper trip plan or app screen against the people present; a party that has grown beyond its permitted headcount — for example picking up a friend partway through the trip — is out of compliance even if the total stays under the 8-person cap, because the names on file did not change. Permit enforcement in the Gorge and Lakeshore zones, which are not quota-limited, still requires a valid backcountry permit for any overnight stay outside the campgrounds; the absence of a quota there does not waive the permit requirement itself.

## Special-use permits

Drone flight is prohibited everywhere in the park without a research permit countersigned by the superintendent — recreational drone use is not permitted at any location or season. A research drone permit application must include the requesting institution, the specific study area, and a flight window no longer than 14 days; the superintendent's countersignature is required before the permit is valid regardless of any other park approval, and an uncountersigned application is treated as denied. There is no application fee for the drone research permit itself, but the requesting institution must carry its own liability insurance naming the Meridian Parks Authority, the same insurance standard applied to larger commercial film crews below.

Commercial filming and photography require a special-use permit at a $250 application fee with a 21-day review; the wildlife program can attach conditions or deny locations during sensitive seasons. A commercial crew of more than six people, or any shoot using drones, generators, or off-trail staging, must also carry proof of liability insurance naming the Meridian Parks Authority, submitted with the application. Still photographers working alone with hand-held equipment for non-commercial or editorial use do not need this permit.

Weddings and organized ceremonies at Fernline Meadow require a $150 permit booked at least 30 days out, with a 40-guest ceiling and no amplified sound. The meadow holds one ceremony per day; a rehearsal walkthrough the same week is included at no extra charge if arranged in advance with the Fernline desk. Décor and seating must be removed the same day, and open flame (including unity candles) is not permitted under the same restrictions that apply to backcountry fires.

Other special-use permits available at the Fernline desk include small educational group visits (school or scout groups over 15 people, no fee but a activity form is required 10 days ahead) and vendor tables at the visitor center for partner organizations, $40/day, capped at two vendor days per month. Organized athletic events on park trails are not permitted.

Non-drone research permits — vegetation surveys, wildlife monitoring, geological sampling, and similar academic or agency fieldwork — require a $100 application fee with a 14-day review, shorter than the commercial filming review because most research proposals do not need a location-conflict check against scheduled ceremonies or commercial shoots. A research permit is tied to named investigators and a defined study area; adding a new researcher mid-project requires an amendment submitted to the Fernline desk, not a new application, and there is no additional fee for an amendment that does not change the study area or extend the permit's end date.

Commercial guiding and outfitting operators who bring paying clients into the park on a recurring basis — guided hikes, fishing trips, or backcountry trips — need an annual commercial operating permit at $500/year rather than a one-time special-use permit, since a single-trip filming-style permit is not designed for repeat commercial activity. The annual operator permit does not itself waive the 8-person cap on backcountry permits or the High Country quota; a guided trip of more than 8 clients must be split into separate backcountry permits under the operator's commercial account, each drawing from the same nightly quota as any other party, and a guide leading multiple linked permits on the same High Country night must be listed on each one. Operators renew the $500 annual permit at the Fernline desk each year before their first guided trip of the season; a lapsed operator permit found in use during a ranger check is treated as unauthorized commercial activity and cited on the same triplicate form used for other civil infractions.

A one-time special-use event that does not fit filming, weddings, research, or commercial guiding — for example a corporate team-building day-use gathering at the Larkspur day-use lot for more than 40 people — is reviewed case by case at the Fernline desk starting at a base fee of $75 for a group up to 75 people, with review time comparable to the 21-day commercial filming window since it also requires a location-conflict and wildlife-program check.

## Reservations, changes, and refunds

All reservations — Larkspur sites, group sites, and backcountry permits — run through the TrailPass system on a six-month rolling booking window, except group sites (12 months, above). A TrailPass account requires only an email address and a payment method; no residency or membership requirement applies. Group bookings of more than one standard site for the same party (for example, three tent sites for one gathering) can be linked under a single reservation code so the party checks in together, but each linked site is still billed and refunded individually at its own nightly rate.

Cancellations made 72 hours or more before arrival receive a full refund minus a $6 processing fee per booking. Inside 72 hours, the first night (or the first permit night) is forfeited. No-shows forfeit the full booking. When the park closes an area for safety — fire, storm damage, or a wildlife closure — affected bookings are refunded in full automatically, including the processing fee, usually within five business days. A partial closure that affects only some nights of a longer stay (for example, a two-night closure inside a five-night booking) refunds only the closed nights, applied the same way as the full-closure refund. Changing the dates of an existing booking, rather than cancelling it outright, is treated as a new booking against the original: the change itself carries no separate fee, but it is still subject to the six-month window and, for High Country permits, to quota availability on the new dates. Fee disputes go to the TrailPass help desk, not to gate staff, and gate staff cannot issue refunds in any circumstance.

Refunds are returned to the original payment method used at booking; a refund cannot be redirected to a different card or converted to park-store credit, and there is no cash refund option even for a booking that was originally paid in cash at a gate or host cabin — those refunds are issued as a mailed check processed by the TrailPass help desk, which can take longer than the standard five business days. A party that books a High Country backcountry permit and is later bumped off the quota by an administrative correction (a rare case where TrailPass over-released a date) receives an automatic full refund including the processing fee, the same treatment given to a safety closure, and is offered priority placement on the next available release for a comparable date.

Downgrading a High Country permit to a Gorge or Lakeshore zone booking on the same dates — for example if a party decides the exposed ridge conditions are not for them — is processed as a cancellation of the original permit under the standard refund schedule above, followed by a new booking in the non-quota zone; the two transactions are not netted against each other, so a downgrade made inside the 72-hour window still forfeits the first night on the original permit even though the replacement booking is in an unrelated zone. A party that wants to upsize a backcountry permit from fewer than 8 people to exactly 8 can amend the existing permit on the TrailPass app without cancelling it, provided High Country quota room remains for the added nights of use; if it does not, the added members must book a separate linked permit instead, subject to the party-splitting restriction described above only applying to camping together rather than to filing paperwork.

The TrailPass website and app are built to standard screen-reader and keyboard-navigation accessibility guidelines, and the booking flow can be completed without a mouse. Visitors without reliable internet access or a smartphone can complete any TrailPass transaction — entrance passes excepted, which are gate or kiosk purchases only — over the phone through the TrailPass help desk during its published hours, or in person at the Fernline desk during visitor center hours, at no added fee for using either alternate channel. A paper reservation request form is available at the Fernline desk for a visitor who prefers not to use a phone or computer at all; the desk staff enters it into TrailPass on the visitor's behalf and provides a printed confirmation, which functions identically to an app confirmation for gate and ranger checks.
