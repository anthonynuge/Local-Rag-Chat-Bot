# Ranger Operations Manual

## Stations, shifts, and radio protocol

The ranger division works out of two duty stations: the Fernline annex at the
South Gate and the Coldpine station at the north entrance. Summer staffing
runs two overlapping shifts, 06:30–15:00 and 12:00–20:30, so both stations
are double-covered through the afternoon incident peak. Every field ranger
carries a Ridgenet radio. Channel assignments are fixed: **channel 1 is
dispatch**, monitored 24 hours; **channel 3 is day-to-day field operations**;
**channel 7 is reserved for search-and-rescue tactical traffic** and stays
clear otherwise. Location reports use trail marker posts ("Sable 14") rather
than coordinates whenever possible, because posts are unambiguous on a shared
channel. Cell coverage inside the park is effectively zero, so the radio net
is the operational backbone; rangers sign out a satellite messenger as backup
when working alone in the High Country zone.

Radio discipline is not optional. Every ranger checks in with dispatch on
channel 1 at the start of shift and confirms battery status; a duty radio
below half charge is swapped before the ranger leaves the station, not
mid-patrol. Each duty vehicle and each ranger's field pack carries one spare
battery, charged on the station's rotation so no battery sits unused past
two weeks. Traffic on channel 3 follows plain language, not codes — the
division dropped ten-codes years ago because they caused more confusion than
they solved on a channel shared with volunteers who don't drill on them
regularly. Channel 7 opens the moment a hasty search is authorized and closes
only when the incident commander declares the search resolved or suspended;
anyone transmitting non-SAR traffic on channel 7 during an open incident is
corrected on the spot. Equipment standards for a field ranger's daily kit are
set by the chief ranger's office and checked at the start-of-season gear
inspection: radio and spare battery, first-aid kit rated to the ranger's
certification level, bear spray, citation book, incident-report forms, a
paper map of the assigned zone, and a headlamp with spare batteries year
round, since several trail corridors lose direct sun well before official
sunset in the shoulder seasons.

## Medical response protocol

Every permanent ranger holds a current Wilderness First Responder
certification or higher, renewed every two years; seasonal rangers must hold
at least a Wilderness First Aid card before solo patrol and complete the
Wilderness First Responder course during their first season if the division
schedules a session. On any call involving an injury, the responding
ranger's first radio transmission gives mechanism of injury, level of
consciousness, and a rough severity read — stable, serious, or critical —
so dispatch can start the evacuation clock before an examination is even
finished. For anything beyond minor first aid, the ranger reaches medical
control by radio relay through dispatch to the on-call physician who
contracts with the division; medications and interventions outside basic
first-aid scope require that physician's authorization, logged with a
timestamp in the incident record. A critical patient triggers the same
helicopter-request path described in the search and rescue protocol below:
the ranger on scene documents the case for the incident commander, but only
the superintendent or acting superintendent can place the actual request to
Meridian Air Ops. Ground evacuation by litter is the default when flight
conditions aren't met or the patient's condition tolerates the additional
time; litter carries require a minimum of six people rotating in pairs, so a
serious backcountry injury on the Sable Ridge Trail routes through the same
ground-team escalation as a search.

## Search and rescue protocol

Search and rescue at Thornmere is coordinated by the chief ranger, and every
stage of a search follows this section. It is written as one continuous
protocol on purpose: in an actual incident the duty officer reads it top to
bottom as a checklist, and nothing in it is optional unless the incident
commander explicitly waives it and logs the waiver.

A search begins with an overdue report. Most reports arrive at the Fernline
desk or through the dispatch line from a family member, and the first duty is
intake: record who is overdue, the trip plan as the reporting party
understands it, party size, experience, medical conditions, equipment, and
the vehicle description. The duty officer immediately checks TrailPass for a
backcountry itinerary and for overnight-parking registration at the Sable
trailhead, and calls the party's phone even though coverage is unlikely —
messages sometimes land when a hiker crests into a coverage pocket outside
the boundary. The duty officer also checks both campground host logs, because
a surprising fraction of "overdue" parties are found asleep at a campsite
they upgraded to on arrival.

The decision point is fixed: **if the party has not been located within two
hours of the initial report, the duty officer notifies the chief ranger and
launches a hasty search**. Waiting beyond two hours requires the chief
ranger's explicit approval and is appropriate only when the itinerary itself
shows the party is not yet actually overdue — for example, a report made at
noon for a party whose plan has them out at 18:00. The hasty phase is
deliberately light: **a hasty team is two rangers**, moving fast on the
party's stated route, starting from the trailhead end where the vehicle is
found. The hasty team travels with a trauma kit, a Ridgenet radio on channel
7, a satellite messenger, and enough overnight equipment to stay with a
located subject through darkness. Hasty teams do not search off-trail; their
job is the high-probability corridor, marker post by marker post, calling and
sound-sweeping at each junction.

If the hasty team clears the route without contact, the incident escalates to
a full callout. From this point the incident has a named incident commander;
**by default the incident commander is Chief Ranger Ruben Ortega**, with the
senior duty ranger at Coldpine as the designated alternate when Ortega is out
of the park or already committed to another incident. The incident commander
owns the search area definition, resource requests, and all task assignments,
and works from the Fernline operations room, not the field. First actions on
escalation are always the same: freeze and copy the TrailPass quota records
for the affected zone to establish who else is out there, assign a dedicated
family liaison from the non-field staff so the reporting party has exactly
one point of contact, and open an incident record in the IRIS incident
system. Nothing about an active search — subject name, search area, or
progress — is released to media or posted publicly by anyone except the
superintendent's office.

Full-callout resources are added in a defined order, because each addition
carries cost and coordination load. Ground teams come first: off-duty
rangers, then the pre-vetted volunteer list maintained at Coldpine station,
organized into teams of three with one radio each, assigned to segments the
incident commander draws on the wall map. Segment assignments follow the
statistical defaults for the terrain — drainages and trail corridors before
ridgelines for a lost hiker, the reverse for an overdue scrambler on the
Summit Route — but the incident commander adjusts for weather, the party's
known plans, and clues as teams report them. Every clue (a found item, a
footprint line, a sign-in at a backcountry register) is radioed to operations
before it is disturbed, logged in IRIS with time and marker-post location,
and marked on the wall map; searches at Thornmere have been redirected more
than once by a single candy wrapper called in correctly.

**A K9 team is requested from the regional cooperator through Coldpine
station once a search passes twelve hours without a confirmed clue**, and the
request goes out earlier at the incident commander's discretion when the
subject is a child or a party member with a medical condition that shortens
the survivability window. The regional cooperator for K9 and technical-rescue
mutual aid is the Highline County Search and Rescue Team, based roughly
ninety minutes from Coldpine station by road; the division's mutual-aid
agreement with Highline County is renewed annually and reviewed at the same
pre-season meeting where the field academy calendar is set. Scent work is
planned before dawn where possible; handlers get the segment map and the
subject's scent article staged at the trailhead the evening before. Highline
County also fields a swiftwater team on standby for Graywater River
incidents in the Gorge zone during spring runoff, activated through the same
Coldpine request line as the K9 team.

Helicopter support is the most constrained resource and the protocol is
strict. **Only the superintendent — Dana Whitlock — or the formally
designated acting superintendent may request the helicopter from Meridian Air
Ops**; the incident commander prepares the justification but does not place
the request. The aircraft flies only when surface winds on the ridge are
below 30 knots and cloud ceiling is above 500 meters over the search area,
and the pilot's weather call is final and not subject to operational
pressure. Approved uses are, in priority order: extraction of a located
subject with a medical condition that ground evacuation would worsen,
insertion of a ground team above treeline when weather is closing, and aerial
sweep of talus and cliff bands that ground teams cannot cover safely. Routine
aerial searching of forested segments is explicitly not an approved use — the
canopy makes it ineffective, and the flight hours come from a regional pool
shared with two other parks.

Night operations are limited to trail-corridor travel by teams that have
already worked the segment in daylight, plus containment: staffed posts at
trailheads and road junctions with lanterns and air horns sounded on a fixed
schedule. Off-trail night searching is prohibited except for a confirmed
subject location, because the park's terrain converts searchers into subjects
at an unacceptable rate in darkness. Each operational period ends with a
full-team debrief at Fernline, segment coverage marked as thorough, partial,
or nominal in IRIS, and the next period's plan written before the incident
commander stands down for the night.

A search that runs seventy-two hours without a confirmed clue triggers a
formal suspension review. The review is chaired by the superintendent with
the incident commander and a regional office representative, and it weighs
survivability modeling, coverage quality, weather, and family input. A
suspension is a change of posture, not an abandonment: containment stays in
place, trail crews and patrol rangers carry the subject profile, and the
incident remains open in IRIS. Any new clue reactivates the search at the
incident commander's level without a new intake process. When a search
closes — found, suspended, or resolved outside the park — the incident
commander completes the IRIS record within twenty-four hours of closure, and
the division holds a structured debrief within five days, with findings filed
to the regional office and folded into the next season's training plan.

## Wildlife incident response

Wildlife incidents — bear encounters with food reward, moose aggression near
trails, or a mountain lion sighting inside a campground — are logged in IRIS
under the wildlife category and routed to the wildlife program led by Dr.
Imani Cole. Rangers respond to the immediate visitor-safety component only:
clearing the area, posting temporary closure signage from the kit each duty
vehicle carries, and reporting on channel 3. Decisions about hazing,
relocation, trap deployment, or a zone closure belong to the wildlife
program, not the responding ranger, and closures it orders are posted the
same day on TrailPass. The feeding-wildlife citation and its fine amount are
covered in the wildlife safety guidance for visitors.

## Law enforcement and citations

Permanent rangers are cross-trained and commissioned to issue citations for
park-rule violations; seasonal rangers may issue citations only after their
field academy law-enforcement module and always under a permanent ranger's
sign-off during their first season. The most common citations are feeding
wildlife (fine amount set in the wildlife safety guidance for visitors),
off-leash dogs outside the Mirrorbend Lakeshore Path and campgrounds,
unauthorized drone operation — prohibited park-wide regardless of location
or season — and campfire violations outside a metal fire ring or during a
fire restriction. Every citation is written on a numbered triplicate form:
one copy to the visitor, one filed at the issuing ranger's home station, one
forwarded to the Fernline administrative annex for the monthly citation log.
A ranger issuing a citation for a safety-critical violation — most often
feeding a bear or approaching wildlife with a child in tow — documents the
specific hazard observed, not just the rule number, because that narrative is
what supports escalation to prosecution if the same visitor repeats the
behavior. Citations are civil infractions handled through the regional
court; a ranger does not collect payment in the field. Repeat or egregious
wildlife violations, including luring animals for photography, are referred
to the superintendent's office for the park-ban process described in the
wildlife safety guidance.

## Winter operations

The Coldpine Gate closes to vehicles November 1 through April 30, and winter
operations shift almost entirely to the South Gate for that period. The
division consolidates duty staffing at the Fernline annex, and the Coldpine
station itself is checked weekly by a snow-machine patrol rather than staffed
daily; the patrol logs building condition, snow load on the station roof, and
any sign of unauthorized winter camping at Coldpine Campground, which is
closed to vehicle camping in winter but remains open to ski-in and
snowshoe-in backcountry travelers who self-register at the South Gate kiosk.
Winter patrol rangers carry avalanche transceivers above treeline in the
High Country zone and complete an annual refresher on transceiver search
technique before the first snow-machine patrol of the season; the
Thornmere Summit Route and the exposed sections of the Sable Ridge Trail are
the two corridors flagged for avalanche awareness in the winter briefing,
consistent with the Summit Route's closure under a winter storm advisory.
Winter search protocol follows the same two-hour hasty-search trigger and
the same incident-command structure as summer operations; the practical
difference is slower ground-team travel time, a lower helicopter-availability
ceiling given the same 30-knot wind and 500-meter ceiling minimums, and a
shortened operational window driven by early winter darkness rather than any
change to the protocol itself.

## Seasonal staffing and training

The division runs eleven permanent rangers year-round and adds nine seasonal
rangers from May 1 through October 15, hired through the Meridian Parks
Authority's central seasonal pool. Seasonal rangers clear a fitness standard
and a three-week field academy at Fernline before solo patrol, and are not
assigned as hasty team members until they have completed two searches in a
support role. The field academy runs the same three weeks every spring,
timed to finish before the May 1 seasonal start date: week one covers
radio protocol, IRIS documentation, and the wildlife-incident response
procedure; week two covers Wilderness First Aid and the law-enforcement and
citation module; week three is field-based, running mock hasty searches on
the Sable Ridge Trail and Mirrorbend Lakeshore Path corridors and finishing
with a written and practical exam administered by the chief ranger's office.
Permanent rangers rotate through the academy as instructors as part of their
own ongoing training requirement. Winter operations consolidate at the South
Gate after the Coldpine seasonal closure, with the Coldpine station checked
weekly by snow-machine patrol.

## Documentation standards

Every incident touched by a ranger — a citation, a wildlife response, a
medical call, or a search — gets an IRIS record, and the division holds
rangers to a consistent standard for what goes in it: time of first report
in 24-hour format, marker-post or GPS location when available, actions
taken in chronological order, names of any other agency involved such as
Highline County Search and Rescue, and a plain-language outcome line. Search
records additionally carry the intake details, every clue logged with its
time and location, and the resource-escalation timeline, since that timeline
is what the five-day debrief and the annual training plan both draw from.
Records are not considered complete until a second ranger — never the
reporting ranger — reviews them for the required fields; incomplete records
are the most common finding at the structured debrief. Media inquiries about
any incident, open or closed, are always redirected to the superintendent's
office; a field ranger's role is limited to clearing the immediate area and
declining comment, never confirming or denying details to a visitor, a
reporter, or anyone posting to social media from the trailhead.

## Equipment maintenance

Every field ranger's daily kit is inspected item by item at the start-of-season gear inspection, but the division does not treat that as the only checkpoint: trauma kits are restocked immediately after any use regardless of how minor the call was, and are otherwise opened and checked against the kit manifest on the first working day of each month at both stations, with the check logged by initials on a card kept inside the kit case so a supervisor can confirm the cadence at a glance. Bear spray canisters carry a manufacture date on the base, and the division replaces any canister within thirty days of its printed expiration rather than waiting for it to actually lapse in the field; expired canisters are not thrown out but handed to the field academy for use in the week-three defensive-use demonstration, since live discharge in a controlled setting is worth more than a spec sheet. Ridgenet radios and their spare batteries go through the same monthly check as the trauma kits, plus a twice-yearly service by a regional communications technician who visits Fernline in the spring before the seasonal staffing ramp-up and again in the fall after Coldpine Gate closes, testing squelch, battery contacts, and antenna condition on every handset in the fleet rather than only the units flagged as faulty. The vehicle fleet — duty trucks at both stations, plus the snow machines staged at Fernline for winter patrol — is logged in a maintenance binder at each station: mileage or engine hours, fluid checks, and any defect noted by the driver at end of shift, with anything affecting safe operation taken out of rotation the same day rather than scheduled around. Citation books, incident-report pads, and trip-registration forms are restocked from a central supply at Fernline whenever a station's count drops below a two-week reserve, so a ranger is never caught without the paperwork a stop or a report requires.

## Trailhead patrol

Routine trailhead patrol is a standing duty layered on top of incident response, not something rangers do only when nothing else is happening. Each duty shift includes at least one pass through the assigned zone's trailhead lot: the Sable trailhead check runs first in the morning, before 08:00 when the small lot typically fills, to confirm overnight vehicles carry a valid permit hang-tag on the dash and to note any vehicle that has clearly been parked longer than its accompanying permit allows. The Gorge and Lakeshore trailhead signboards — conditions sheet and marker-keyed map — are refreshed twice daily in the summer season, once at shift start and again in early afternoon, so a closure, a trail hazard, or the peregrine-nesting closure between Gorge markers 9 and 11 is reflected before the midday visitor peak rather than left stale from the previous evening. The voluntary trip-registration box at each trailhead is emptied and logged at the same cadence; entries are not cross-checked against anything in real time, but the duty officer pulls the day's slips first during an overdue-report intake, since a filled-out registration slip is often faster to read than working the itinerary from a phone call with a worried family member. Rangers walking a trailhead patrol also do a quick visual sweep of the lot itself — unattended coolers, food left visible in a vehicle, a vehicle that looks abandoned rather than parked — because unsecured food at a trailhead draws the same wildlife problems as an unsecured campsite, just concentrated in a smaller area. A trailhead found without functioning signage, an overflowing registration box, or a permit-hang-tag violation goes into the same shift log a wildlife sighting or a citation would, so the pattern is visible to the chief ranger's office without anyone having to ask.

## Communications backup

The Ridgenet system depends on a repeater sited on the Thornmere massif, and the division treats repeater failure as a standing contingency rather than a rare emergency. If a ranger loses channel contact in the field, the first fallback is line-of-sight simplex on channel 3 directly to another patrol unit or to whichever station is closer, since a repeater outage does not take down radio-to-radio range between two rangers working the same drainage. If that fails, the satellite messenger each High Country ranger carries is the next step, used to send a preset check-in or emergency message to the duty station rather than attempting an improvised text, because preset messages are what the station's messenger account is actually monitored for. A ranger who cannot raise anyone by either method falls back to the last-resort rule: return to the nearest station or trailhead with a working landline rather than continuing the patrol, and report in person. Both stations keep a printed backup contact sheet — regional communications technician, Highline County Search and Rescue duty line, Meridian Air Ops, and the on-call physician — taped inside the radio room door, so a repeater outage during an active incident doesn't strand the duty officer trying to remember or look up a number under pressure. A confirmed repeater outage is itself logged in IRIS as an operational event, with the outage window noted, because a search or a medical call that ran during a known outage is read differently at the five-day debrief than one that didn't.

## Mutual-aid logistics

Highline County Search and Rescue is the primary mutual-aid partner for K9, technical rescue, and swiftwater response, but the division's mutual-aid file at Fernline also carries an agreement with the Fern Hollow volunteer fire department for structure and vehicle fires at either campground, since the division's own equipment does not extend past a vehicle fire extinguisher and initial evacuation. Both agreements are reviewed and re-signed at the same pre-season meeting where the field academy calendar is set, and any change in either partner's roster of qualified personnel or equipment is supposed to be reported to the chief ranger's office as it happens rather than waiting for the annual review. Requesting mutual aid from either partner follows the same shape regardless of which one: the request goes out from the Coldpine station duty line, is logged in IRIS with the time of the call and the resource requested, and the incident commander — or, before a full callout exists, the duty officer — briefs the arriving mutual-aid team at the trailhead or station rather than sending them straight into the field without an orientation to the terrain and the current plan. Highline County crews responding to Thornmere operate under their own agency's safety rules but take tactical direction from the incident commander for the duration of the incident, a point specified in the written agreement precisely because a multi-agency scene without a single point of tactical control is where confusion costs time. When a mutual-aid resource is released, its return leg and estimated availability for a subsequent request are noted in the incident record, since a K9 team or a swiftwater team released from one incident is not necessarily available again for several hours.

## After-action process

The five-day structured debrief already required for a suspended or closed search is the most formal after-action review the division runs, but it is not the only one. Any incident involving an injury, a helicopter request, a mutual-aid activation, or a citation escalated toward prosecution gets a short "hot wash" — an informal same-day conversation among the rangers directly involved, held before the end of shift while details are still fresh, covering what worked, what didn't, and anything that needs fixing before the next similar call. The hot wash is not itself an IRIS entry; it exists to surface things quickly, and anything substantive from it is folded into the incident record by whichever ranger owns the write-up. Beyond the incident level, the chief ranger's office runs a quarterly review that pulls the citation log, the wildlife-incident log, and any search or medical records from the period and looks for pattern rather than individual cases — a marker post section generating repeat wildlife-feeding citations, a trailhead where overdue reports cluster, a piece of equipment that failed more than once. Findings from the quarterly review feed the following year's field academy curriculum the same way a search debrief does, so a training gap identified in July does not wait until the next incident to get addressed. Seasonal rangers finishing their contract each October also sit a short exit conversation with their station supervisor, separate from any incident debrief, covering what the season's training didn't prepare them for; that feedback is filed alongside the quarterly reviews and is the input most directly credited with changes to the field academy's week-three field exercises over the years.
